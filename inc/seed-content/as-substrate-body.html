Music has an identification problem. Administrative codes aren't the answer.

A short read on why music files need fingerprints, not just name tags.

Section 1

The setup

The music industry has 253 million tracks on streaming services. About 106,000 new tracks get added every day. The Mechanical Licensing Collective in the United States alone holds hundreds of millions of dollars in royalties that have nothing to pay them to. Every other collection society in every other country has a similar pile.

The pile keeps growing. The reason isn't that the platforms don't try, or that the lawyers don't care, or that the technology isn't there. The reason is that the identifiers the music industry uses to track works don't reliably identify them.

When a song gets recorded, distributed, played, and paid for, the chain runs on codes. There's a code for the recording, a code for the underlying composition, a code for the artist as a person, a code for the commercial release. These codes get issued by administrative bodies, assigned by human beings, typed into databases, and propagated through systems that don't always agree with each other.

By the time a stream gets reported and a royalty has to flow, the file's codes might not match the registry's codes might not match the platform's codes. The royalty goes into an unmatched pool. The pool gets bigger every quarter.

AI-generated music makes this worse, not because it's a different category of problem, but because it's pouring more files into a system that was already failing to identify what was there.

Section 2

The analogy

Consider how we identify people.

A person could carry a name tag with their identity printed on it. The name tag is administrative: someone wrote it, someone handed it to them, and if it gets lost, dropped, or duplicated, the system has no way to verify who's really who.

A person also has fingerprints. Fingerprints exist from birth. They aren't issued by anyone. They can't be transferred. Two people can't accidentally have the same one. You can verify a fingerprint just by looking at the print itself. You don't need to call an authority and ask whether the print is valid.

Music identifiers today work like name tags. A code gets assigned by an agency, written into a registry, and passed along the distribution chain. When the codes don't agree, or when the file shows up without a code at all, the system tries to guess what it's looking at.

Music identifiers as a substrate would work like fingerprints. The identity is computed from the work itself, exists the moment the file does, and can't be transferred to a different file. Two recordings can't accidentally claim the same identifier. Verification doesn't require calling a registry. Anyone with the file can compute the fingerprint and compare.

The administrative codes don't go away. They just become labels attached to fingerprints, the way a name tag is now attached to a person who already has a unique fingerprint regardless.

Section 3

What the substrate is

Four properties define a substrate worth building.

Self-issuing. The identifier exists the moment the file does. There's no agency to apply to, no allocation block to wait for, no registration step. A producer in Lagos and a producer in Tokyo can both produce identifiers for their work the same minute, and neither has to coordinate with the other.

Collision-free. Two different works produce two different identifiers, with mathematical certainty. Today's administrative codes can collide, get reissued, or get assigned twice because nobody noticed. Cryptographic identifiers don't have this failure mode. The math eliminates it.

Authorship and identity in one. Today, knowing what a recording is and knowing who owns it require querying different systems that may or may not agree. A signed identifier carries both at once. The signature proves who attests to the work; the identifier is the work. Anyone verifying one verifies the other.

Backward-compatible by design. All the existing codes don't get retired. They become aliases of the new identifiers. A recording that already has a code keeps using it; the new identifier just lives underneath as the source of truth when the codes disagree.

The cryptographic primitives that make this work have been around for decades. They secure financial transactions, software signing, and government identity systems. Bringing them into music is engineering work, not invention.

Section 4

Why this matters

Three groups feel the consequences differently.

For the people who run royalty collection. The Mechanical Licensing Collective and its equivalents around the world spend enormous resources trying to match streaming reports to rights holders. Most of the unmatched pool isn't malice or bad faith. It's identifier failure. With substrate identifiers, the matching problem becomes a verification check. The pool starts shrinking because the work doesn't need to be matched. It identifies itself.

For artists. Royalties get paid faster and more accurately. The work the producer signed at creation carries the identifying information all the way through the chain. A track that lands at a streaming service in Berlin and gets played in São Paulo doesn't lose its way to Buenos Aires through a mistyped code or a missing field. The chain holds.

For everyone planning the music industry's next ten years. The volume of new music is going to keep climbing. AI-generated content adds to it. New genres add to it. Global access keeps adding markets. A system that already can't keep up at 100,000 new tracks a day is not going to start keeping up at 200,000. The substrate is the only architecture that works without scaling administrative cost linearly with volume.

The win isn't bureaucratic. It's that artists get paid for what they made, faster.

Section 5

The shift

The choice is between two architectures.

The current architecture asks: "Did the right code make it through this handoff?" The answer gets less reliable every step. By the time a royalty is calculated, the chain of custody has passed through five or six systems that might not agree, and the cost of one wrong field is paid by whoever's name was supposed to be on it.

The substrate architecture asks: "Does this file's identity verify against its signature?" The answer doesn't degrade. It either checks out or it doesn't, regardless of how many systems the file passed through, regardless of which territory it ended up in, regardless of whether the original distributor went out of business.

One of these scales. The other has been failing slowly for decades, and the failure is getting more expensive every year.

The industry will pick. The question is whether it picks deliberately, or whether the unmatched pool gets large enough to force the question.

Section 6

The economics of identification

The economics tell the same story as the technology.

Administrative infrastructure scales linearly. Every new track requires a code to be issued, registered, and propagated. Every cross-border release requires the codes to be reconciled. Every catalog acquisition requires the metadata to be cleaned. The cost of running this system goes up with the number of tracks, and the number of tracks is exploding.

Cryptographic infrastructure scales with computers, not clerks. A new identifier costs nothing to compute. Verification is a millisecond operation. Cross-border doesn't require reconciliation because the identity is the same identity regardless of which border it crossed. A catalog acquisition's metadata work becomes a check on which signed records came with the catalog, not a research project.

The unmatched pool is a tax. Every dollar in it is a payment owed to a specific creator that the system couldn't identify. As long as the architecture is administrative, the pool grows. Once the architecture is cryptographic, the pool starts shrinking because the matching problem dissolves at its source.

The industry will choose between paying for clerks or paying for cryptography. Clerks have been the answer for decades and the answer is getting worse. The math points the other direction. The first major collection society to migrate sets the pattern; everyone else follows because they're the only ones still bleeding.
